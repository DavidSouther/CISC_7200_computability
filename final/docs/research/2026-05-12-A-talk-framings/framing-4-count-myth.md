# Framing 4: The Count and the Myth

## Verdict: SOMEWHAT UNIQUE

The specific phrasing "the Count and the Myth" is almost certainly the author's own coinage; a search of the literature returns no prior use as a named duality. The underlying idea, however, that there is a fundamental tension between a countable/empirical pole and a linguistic/narrative pole, is one of the most well-trodden dualities in Western thought. What is novel here is the application of that duality to Rice-Shapiro, falsification, and program-versus-hardware in a single rhetorical spine. The component oppositions are familiar; the assembly is the author's.

## Closest analogues in the literature

- Mythos vs logos. The canonical Greek duality between story/imagination and rational/numerical account. The narrative-vs-number polarity is exactly the territory the talk wants to occupy, and audiences trained in the humanities will hear "Count and Myth" as a paraphrase of this pair.
- C. P. Snow, The Two Cultures (1959). The split between literary intellectuals and scientists is the modern academic version of the same opposition, framed institutionally rather than metaphysically.
- Vico, verum-factum and poetic wisdom. Vico already pairs mathematics (knowable because made) with sapienza poetica (myth, metaphor, narrative) as the two routes by which humans know what they have made. This is the closest historical precedent for treating number and myth as complementary epistemic poles rather than as enemies.
- Walter Ong, Orality and Literacy. Ong contrasts the additive, narrative, mnemonic structures of oral cultures with the analytic, list-based, numerical structures that writing enables. Number versus story is literally his subject.
- Lakoff and Nunez, Where Mathematics Comes From. Argues that mathematics itself is built from embodied metaphors, and explicitly attacks "the Romance of Mathematics" as a myth. The book treats number as continuous with narrative rather than opposed to it.
- N. Katherine Hayles, How We Think. Frames database and narrative as "natural symbionts," not enemies. This is the closest contemporary match for the talk's intended move: pairing the countable (database, observation) with the narrative (myth, claim) as cooperating modes.
- Brian Cantwell Smith, On the Origin of Objects. Attacks the view of computation as purely abstract/mathematical and insists on its embedded, physical character. Directly relevant to the "program (myth) versus hardware (count)" leg of the framing.
- Luciano Floridi, Levels of Abstraction. Provides a formal vocabulary for how the same physical substrate supports multiple linguistic descriptions, which is exactly the move the program/hardware framing wants to make.
- Hardware/software dualism as Cartesian analogue. The mapping of mind:body :: software:hardware is widespread in philosophy of mind and AI; the talk's Descartes connection is well-precedented rather than novel.
- Wittgenstein, saying versus showing. Mathematics shows what cannot be said; closely adjacent to a Rice-Shapiro reading that separates what programs do (observable count) from what claims about them assert (myth).

## Assessment

The author should expect at least one audience member to map "Count and Myth" onto mythos/logos within thirty seconds. That is a feature, not a bug: the framing borrows the gravity of an ancient pairing. The genuinely new contribution is the claim that Rice-Shapiro is a theorem about exactly this divide (the undecidability of crossing from syntactic count to semantic myth) and that the history of falsification rehearses the same crossing. Those two anchors are where the framing earns its keep; the duality itself is rented from a long tradition.
