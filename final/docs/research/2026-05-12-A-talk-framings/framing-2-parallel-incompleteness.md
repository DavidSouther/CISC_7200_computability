# Framing 2: Parallel Incompleteness (Math vs Empirical Science)

## Verdict: COMMON

The framing of mathematical limits (Goedel, Turing, Rice) as parallel to scientific limits (Popper, Quine-Duhem, Lakatos) is well-trodden ground in philosophy of mathematics and popular science writing. It is not a novel synthesis. However, the specific pedagogical pairing of Rice's theorem with Popperian falsifiability, and the use of Shapiro's structuralism as the recovery mechanism, is less commonly packaged and offers room for originality.

## Authors and works that draw this parallel

1. Gregory Chaitin is the central figure. He explicitly argues that algorithmic information theory shows mathematics is "quasi-empirical" and must adopt scientific methodology. He cites Popper's "Logic of Scientific Discovery" directly and proposes that mathematicians treat unprovable conjectures (P vs NP, Riemann) as physicists treat hypotheses: as axioms adopted on pragmatic/empirical grounds. See "Doing Mathematics Differently" and his Enciclopedia Italiana essay on foundations.

2. Imre Lakatos is the historical pivot. "Proofs and Refutations" (dedicated to Popper) applies a Popperian conjectures-and-refutations methodology to mathematics. Lakatos coined "quasi-empiricism" for mathematics. Chaitin explicitly builds on Lakatos.

3. Hilary Putnam ("What is mathematical truth?") and Reuben Hersh also developed quasi-empirical philosophy of mathematics in the 1970s-1980s.

4. Roger Penrose ("The Emperor's New Mind") uses Goedel and Turing to argue for limits on what computational/scientific accounts can capture, though his conclusion (non-algorithmic consciousness) differs from the recovery-of-knowledge framing.

5. Douglas Hofstadter ("Goedel, Escher, Bach") thematizes self-reference and incompleteness but does not foreground Popper specifically.

6. Scott Aaronson's "PHYS771 Lecture 3: Goedel, Turing, and Friends" is a widely-cited computability lecture that gestures at these epistemic parallels.

7. Panu Raatikainen has published critical analyses of Chaitin's overreach in interpreting incompleteness as an empirical fact, which itself shows the parallel framing is mainstream enough to attract critique.

8. The arXiv paper "On the Inherent Incompleteness of Scientific Theories" (Activitas Nervosa Superior) explicitly connects Goedel/Tarski to Quine-Duhem and observational/theoretical distinction failure, drawing the parallel directly.

## Close-but-not-identical framings

- Penrose-Lucas argument: uses Goedel to limit AI/mechanism, not to set up a Popperian recovery story.
- Lakatos's research programmes: applies Popperian structure to science, not mathematics' limits per se.
- Stewart Shapiro's structuralism: links mathematical knowledge to scientific knowledge methodologically (Shapiro argues epistemic problems in math are "no more troublesome" than scientific ones), which is structurally the recovery move the author wants, but Shapiro does not frame this against Goedel/Turing/Rice as the failure mode being recovered from.
- "Computability, Goedel's incompleteness theorem, and an inherent limit on the predictability of evolution" (J. Royal Society Interface) applies computability limits to empirical science but as constraint, not as parallel.

## Assessment for the talk

The general "math has its own Popper-shaped problem" move is COMMON, and Chaitin owns it. What is less common and more defensible as fresh framing:

- Pairing Rice (not just Goedel) with Popper; Rice rarely appears in this discussion.
- Using Shapiro's structuralism (rather than Lakatos/Chaitin quasi-empiricism) as the recovery mechanism. This is a less-worn move.
- A pedagogical synthesis aimed at a computability course audience rather than a philosophy of math audience.

The author should expect a knowledgeable audience member to invoke Chaitin or Lakatos. Acknowledging Chaitin explicitly and positioning the Shapiro-recovery angle as the novel contribution would strengthen the talk.
